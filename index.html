A Queen Forgotten by History
Mayur Chhoriya  ·  March 2026

In 1900, the Travancore royal family was facing an existential crisis regarding its lineage because there was no female heir to continue the matrilineal line. The anxious Maharani Lakshmi Bayi sought the approval of Maharajah Moolam Thirunal to adopt a girl instead. At the request of Raja Ravi Varma, a royal-turned-painter, she adopted his 2 granddaughters into the royal lineage.

They essentially became "property of the state" from the age of five. They were separated from their biological parents and placed under the care of strict tutors and guardians who controlled every minute of their day to ensure the "purity" of the royal line.

Subsequently, a series of deaths in the royal family alterted the line of succession, almost unexpectedly placing a 6-year old girl as the Senior Maharani of Travancore. While Maharajah Moolam Thirunal Rama Varma continued to reign, the young girl became central to Travancore's matrilineal succession system.

That 6-year-old girl was Sethu Lakshmi Bayi - one of the two adopted princesses (the other girl, Sethu Parvathi Bayi, became the Junior Maharani). And if her name doesn't sound familiar, that is precisely the sad point.

For someone who presided over one of India's most prosperous princely states as Regent from 1924 to 1931, she is remarkably missing from public memory.

Manu S. Pillai's The Ivory Throne uncovers this largely forgotten world. It reveals a political and social order very different from the conventional image of Indian monarchy. Travancore followed a matrilineal inheritance system called Marumakkathayam. Lineage and inheritance passed through the female line, which meant the Maharani was far more than a ceremonial figure. A ruler was not necessarily succeeded by his son but by his sister's son.

The World of Ritual and Control

The Senior Maharani lived a life that was regulated in every sense of the word. Daily routines at Sundara Vilasam Palace were governed by the 12-volume Palace Manual. Its rules covered even the most mundane tasks - from the brushing of royal pearls (yes, her teeth were referred to as royal pearls) under the supervision of court dentists, to sleeping in the proper posture at night. Every morning, the Maharani's staff guided her and her cousins to begin the royal bath, known as pallineerattu. She wore crisp mundus and changed them three times a day, each freshly laundered. She was always accompanied by the constant presence of devoted servants. Even something as ordinary as walking around the palace required a group of guards and servants, called pattakkars, to accompany her.

When Sethu Lakshmi Bayi turned 10, it was to be seen which eligible bachelor from the limited circle of Travancore's aristocratic families would win her approval. After she made her choice, the chosen groom, Rama Verma, was taken to Bhakti Vilas, the Dewan's official residence, where an English doctor, Col. James, performed a thorough medical examination. After receiving the doctor's approval, the Resident was informed, and through him, the Madras Government. Eventually, when the Maharani was ready for marriage, astrologers determined an auspicious day for the ceremony. Meanwhile, the royal family's head priest was assigned to tutor the young consort in the dos and don'ts of lovemaking.

The Maharani's husband was not the Maharajah; but merely a consort - socially and politically subordinate to his wife. This status difference was clear everywhere in state protocols. The Maharani's full title reflected her grand status: Her Highness Sree Padmanabhasevini, Vanchidharma Vardhini, Raja Rajeshwari, Maharani Pooradam Thirunal Sethu Lakshmi Bayi, Maharaja, Attingal Mootha Thampuran, Companion of the Imperial Order of the Crown of India, Maharani Regent of Travancore.

In contrast, her husband was just Mr. Rama Varma Valiya Koil Thampuran. His main role was to support his wife by creating the next generation. The rank difference was not just symbolic; it was institutional. The consort could not sit in her presence unless invited. He was not allowed to ride in the same carriage as the Maharani, as that would imply equality. When the Maharani entered the capital, she received a 21 gun salute, while the consort received only a rifle salute. At royal banquets, the Maharani and her children were served four types of dessert, while consorts received just two, and ordinary guests had to settle for one. As she stepped down from the first floor, hundreds of servants would greet her with seven namaskars, bowing from their heads to the ground. Even in death, the rules held firm. The palace was reserved for royalty to die. If the consort were nearing death, he had to be carried out to a private building since a commoner could not die within royal walls. His children could not attend his funeral because royalty don't participate in the ceremonies of the lesser-born.

Caste rules were strictly observed inside the palace. Every servant was chosen by caste: ordinary staff came from respectable Nair families, while personal attendants were Brahmins. Distinguished guests and visitors were trained to follow court protocol. Even the Maharani could not change these protocols. This carefully structured world created a remarkable contradiction. The Maharani had immense authority over millions but lived within a complex framework of restrictions that left little room for personal freedom. These rituals were tools to construct and reinforce her legitimacy.

Many monarchies - from Louis XIV's Versailles to the Qing court in Beijing - used ritual in the same way. Power wasn't just exercised; it had to be performed. The palace became a stage where hierarchy was constantly rehearsed so that the political order looked sacred and unquestionable.

Palace Rivalry and Intrigue

The tension between Travancore's two princesses, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi and Sethu Parvathi Bayi, started almost as soon as they were adopted into the royal blood in 1900.

The elder sister, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi, became the Senior Maharani, and the younger, Sethu Parvathi Bayi, the Junior Maharani. But the Travancore's matrilineal system made life far less simple as succession would not pass through the Senior Maharani but through the children of the Junior Maharani. It meant that although Sethu Lakshmi Bayi later ruled as Regent, the future Maharajah was expected to be Sethu Parvathi Bayi's son, Chithira Thirunal Balarama Varma.

Naturally this created a tense and suspicious atmosphere in the palace. People gradually began to take sides, and the court slowly split into rival camps. Pillai describes episodes where supporters of the Junior Maharani were accused of bringing kshudraprayogam from the Malabar coast to perform rituals against the Regent. The book describes a "very favourite form of kshudraprayogam" which involved burying objects to curse a victim. In one specific instance, a bracelet was found buried under the threshold of the Senior Maharani's door at her palace. A report to the Government of India detailed a secret ceremony where nine or eleven Nambutiri Brahmins were housed in the palace in strict secrecy. To perform the rite, the granite floor of the old palace was lifted to dig a "square hole in the bare earth," and large quantities of ghee were imported specifically for the ritual.

These allegations were taken so seriously by British authorities that in the late 1920s, they issued strict orders for the removal of Kochukunji from the palace due to her role in these palace intrigues. The British Resident at the time, Lt. Col. Crosthwaite, noted that while one might dismiss "black magic" as nonsense, there was no doubt that these activities had "very seriously affected the Palace" and were becoming public knowledge.

Sethu Parvathi Bayi was definitely a woman of defiance. For instance, she was determined to travel to Europe, which was strictly forbidden for Travancore royals due to the "Black Water" (Kala Pani) taboo. Defying the orthodox priests, she went anyway. Upon her return, rather than performing the humiliating and expensive purification rituals (which often involved being "reborn" through a golden cow), she simply refused, signaling her resistance against rigid priesthood.

She often attempted to overthrow Sethu Lakshmi Bayi's regency and sent complaints about her to the Viceroy. To her dismay, the Viceroy instead decorated Sethu Lakshmi Bayi with the Order of the Crown of India. Many at the time believed that her son remained strongly influenced and overshadowed by her. British records from the time mention propaganda battles inside the court and later also note her support for a more assertive and conservative political stance after her son became the Maharaja.

After Sethu Parvathi Bayi's son became the Maharaja, people suspected an intense relationship between her and Sir C.P. Ramaswami Aiyar (a lawyer from Madras). Their bond was so close that rumors of a "boudoir" influence began to circulate, suggesting that he was the real power behind the throne. This again created a massive rift with the Senior Maharani, who viewed C.P. as a dangerous influence who was pulling the young Maharajah away from the traditional, balanced style of governance she had worked to maintain during her Regency.

The Victorian Morality

Traditionally, the institution of marriage in Travancore was not based on strict monogamy and instead relied on the matrilineal Marumakkathayam system, where family identity and property passed through women, not men. Within this system, the practice of Sambandham gave women a level of sexual and social freedom that was very different from what Europeans had seen or experienced. A woman did not "marry into" another family; she remained in charge of her ancestral home (Taravad), and her partners were essentially guests without legal rights to her, her property, or her children. Because property passed from a man to his sister's children, the Western idea of proving who the father was didn't matter much.

Similarly, this indigenous autonomy extended even to physical appearance, as women of the royal household traditionally did not wear blouses or upper garments. This was not a sign of immodesty but rather a cultural norm. Bare-chestedness was a sign of respect toward deities and social superiors.

The British were scandalized by these customs, viewing both the lack of formal marriage and the traditional dress as signs of "moral decline" and "savagery."

Consequently, colonial officials and missionaries pressured the Travancore royals and the Nair nobility to adopt Western-style modesty and monogamy to address this. Over time, the Western-educated Malayali men started adopting this Oriental lens and began to feel embarrassed about their traditional customs. They began to press for new laws that forced women to cover up and made men the heads of the family. The tragic result was that women traded their unique matrilineal authority for a patriarchal order, leaving them arguably more restricted than their ancestors.

Travancore under the Regency

The title of the book comes a magnificent throne carved entirely from ivory by the artisans of Travancore and gifted to the Maharajah. The Regent, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi, rarely felt comfortable on it. She preferred her simple wooden desk and chair, viewing the ivory throne as a heavy burden of duty that eventually cost her her family and her peace of mind.

Sethu Lakshmi Bayi's rule as Regent from 1924 to 1931 changed Travancore in many ways. She abolished caste-based restrictions meant for lower castes and enforced a strict policy against segregation in education based on religion or caste. Midday meals were introduced for children from poorer backgrounds, and educational grants were expanded. Her administration opened colleges and government offices to women and hired trained teachers from Europe to enhance the educational system. In 1926, she abolished the devadasi system.

Minorities also found a place in her government. Despite a no-no from conservatives, she appointed Christians and other marginalized groups to senior positions.

Under her rule, Travancore became one of the most prosperous princely states in India. Revenue grew steadily, and which were wisely spent on building roads, bridges, hospitals, telephone and electricity lines reaching villages that had never seen it before.

Travancore was never isolated from the world, thanks to its location on the peninsula. It became a gateway that attracted merchants, scholars, missionaries, and travelers from across Europe and Asia, who brought them with them new ideas, art and avenues for commerce. The most vivid example is Raja Ravi Varma, who combined European realism with Indian mythological themes without any contradiction in his art.

One of her most enduring contributions was the Village Panchayat Act of 1925 - which laid the foundation for the strong tradition of participatory panchayat governance seen in Kerala today.

During the early years of her regency, which coincided with the Vaikom Satyagraha, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi met Mahatma Gandhi in 1925 to discuss the lower castes' demand to use the roads around the Vaikom Mahadeva Temple. Gandhi was struck by her quiet, unadorned appearance (she received him in a plain white Kerala sari rather than royal jewels) which reflected the restrained style of her rule. Soon after their meeting, a royal proclamation opened almost all the public roads to all castes. Her rule drew praise from both nationalists and British officials. In 1929, the viceroy referred to her rule as a period of great progress, and later Louis Mountbatten remarked that no one who met her could forget her, calling her a "shining example to womanhood as a great queen and a great woman."

The End of a Royal World

Despite the prosperity during her regency, palace politics had the better of Sethu Lakshmi Bayi. By her forties she had become increasingly isolated within the court, and after the regency ended she withdrew from active involvement in state affairs. For a while, she did continue overseeing matters related to the Sreepadom estate, but in 1939 the Maharajah took control and placed it in a trust. This effectively stripped Sethu Lakshmi Bayi of her traditional holdings as the Senior Maharani. Her complaints to the Viceroy brought her no relief. Around this period, a fire destroyed all official papers and records related to her regency.

India became independent in 1947, and in 1949 the princely state of Travancore merged with the Republic of India, bringing centuries of monarchy to an end. A powerful moment captured this transition. In 1957, after a communist government was elected in Kerala, her own palace servants formed a union in the palace, shouted slogans, removed the Travancore flag, and replaced it with a communist banner on the palace roof. For a dynasty built on elaborate protocols and hierarchy, the end was both clear and inevitable.

While Rama Varma was furious and vowed to get deep into the matter, the Maharani decided to leave the palace - a fate she had been contemplating for some time. As they drove out of the gates, Rama Varma asked his wife if she wanted a last look at their home. The Maharani, however, refused. Deep down, she knew she would not return. As she left in a car, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi was no longer the grand queen she once was, but a wife, a grandmother, a private citizen.

On the way to the railway station she stopped at the gates of the sacred Sri Padmanabhaswamy Temple to offer a final prayer to the deity under whose name she had once ruled Travancore.

She then gifted her palaces to the government. Her summer palace became an agricultural university, her official residence became a medical research facility, and her beach became a public tourist destination.

The Quiet End of a Queen

The former queen moved to Bangalore and gracefully let go of the many titles she had once carried. Life became ordinary in ways she had never imagined or experienced. She rode the public bus, stood in long queues, sat in public parks, and sometimes went to the local markets herself. When for the first time her daughter went shopping, she realised something surprising - she did not even know the value of a rupee, because she had never handled money before.

In a way, she had finally found the liberty that the palace had denied her for decades. Yet the change brought mixed emotions. Her grandson would later say, "Being a member of the royal family was like being a favourite bird in a golden cage. You were watched and humored and spoilt and loved. It was only when you were alone with yourself that you remembered the cage."

When the government ended the Privy Purse, she stopped receiving allowances (though they were restored shortly before her death after a long legal battle). When the Maharani died, it was largely unnoticed. The woman who once ruled five million people and received ceremonial gun salutes was cremated in a public crematorium with little attention.

Reflections on Power and Impermanence

Power is fragile. For a kingdom that lasted a thousand years, authority, titles, and respect may seem permanent while they exist, but history knows well how quickly they can collapse when the foundations beneath them start to wobble.

Sethu Lakshmi Bayi's story reflects that reality. For someone who gracefully gave up everything that was slipping away, her life tells a story of the impermanence of it all - and of a certain freedom in letting go.

The Ivory Throne is not just about queens and kings, but about the human story behind every monarchy - one that is full of insecurity, ambition, rivalry, dignity, and loss.
