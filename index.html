A Queen Forgotten by History
In 1900, the Travancore royal family was facing an existential crisis regarding its lineage because there was no female heir to continue the matrilineal line.
Mayur Chhoriya  ·  March 2026
+

In 1900, the Travancore royal family was facing an existential crisis regarding its lineage because there was no female heir to continue the matrilineal line. The anxious Maharani Lakshmi Bayi sought the approval of Maharajah Moolam Thirunal to adopt a girl instead. At the request of Raja Ravi Varma, a royal-turned-painter, she adopted his 2 granddaughters into the royal lineage. 

They essentially became "property of the state" from the age of five. They were separated from their biological parents and placed under the care of strict tutors and guardians who controlled every minute of their day to ensure the "purity" of the royal line.

Subsequently, a series of deaths in the royal family altered the line of succession, almost unexpectedly placing a 6-year old girl as the Senior Maharani of Travancore. While Maharajah Moolam Thirunal Rama Varma continued to reign, the young girl became central to Travancore's matrilineal succession system.

That 6-year-old girl was Sethu Lakshmi Bayi - one of the two adopted princesses (the other girl, Sethu Parvathi Bayi, became the Junior Maharani). And if her name doesn't sound familiar, that is precisely the sad point. 

For someone who presided over one of India's most prosperous princely states as Regent from 1924 to 1931, she is remarkably missing from public memory. 

Manu S. Pillai's The Ivory Throne uncovers this largely forgotten world. It reveals a political and social order very different from the conventional image of Indian monarchy. Travancore followed a matrilineal inheritance system called 'Marumakkathayam'. Lineage and inheritance passed through the female line, which meant the Maharani was far more than a ceremonial figure.

The World of Ritual and Control

The Senior Maharani lived a life that was "royal" in every sense of the word. Daily routines at Sundara Vilasam Palace were governed by the 12-volume Palace Manual. Its rules covered even the mundane — from the brushing of royal pearls (yes, her teeth were referred to as royal pearls) under the supervision of court dentists to sleeping in the proper posture at night.

The Maharani's full title reflected her grand status: 'Her Highness Sree Padmanabhasevini, Vanchidharma Vardhini, Raja Rajeshwari, Maharani Pooradam Thirunal Sethu Lakshmi Bayi, Maharaja, Attingal Mootha Thampuran, Companion of the Imperial Order of the Crown of India, Maharani Regent of Travancore.'

In contrast, her husband was just 'Mr. Rama Varma Valiya Koil Thampuran'. The rank difference was not just symbolic; it was institutional. The consort could not sit in her presence unless invited. He was not allowed to ride in the same carriage as the Maharani, as that would imply 'equality'.

Palace Rivalry and Intrigue

The tension between Travancore's two princesses, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi and Sethu Parvathi Bayi, started almost as soon as they were adopted into the royal blood in 1900.

The elder sister, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi, became the Senior Maharani, and the younger, Sethu Parvathi Bayi, the Junior Maharani. But the Travancore's matrilineal system made life far less simple as succession would not pass through the Senior Maharani but through the children of the Junior Maharani.

Travancore under the Regency

Sethu Lakshmi Bayi's rule as Regent from 1924 to 1931 changed Travancore in many ways. She abolished "special schools" meant for lower castes and enforced a strict policy against segregation in education based on religion or caste. In 1926, she abolished the devadasi system.

Under her rule, Travancore became one of the most prosperous princely states in India. Revenue grew steadily, wisely spent on building roads, bridges, hospitals, telephone and electricity lines reaching villages that had never seen it before.

During the early years of her regency, which coincided with the Vaikom Satyagraha, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi met Mahatma Gandhi in 1925 to discuss the lower castes' demand to use the roads around the Vaikom Mahadeva Temple. Gandhi was struck by her quiet, unadorned appearance — she received him in a plain white Kerala sari rather than royal jewels.

The Quiet End of a Queen

In 1957, after a communist government was elected in Kerala, her own "loyal servants" formed a union in the palace, shouted slogans, removed the Travancore flag, and replaced it with a communist banner on the palace roof. The Maharani decided to leave. As she left in a car, Sethu Lakshmi Bayi was no longer the grand queen she once was, but a wife, a grandmother, a "common citizen".

She moved to Bangalore and gracefully let go of the many titles she had once carried. Life became ordinary in ways she had never imagined. She rode the public bus, stood in long queues, sat in public parks. Her grandson would later say, "Being a member of the royal family was like being a favorite bird in a golden cage. You were watched and humored and spoilt and loved. It was only when you were alone with yourself that you remembered the cage."

When the Maharani died in 1985, it was largely unnoticed. The woman who once ruled five million people and received ceremonial gun salutes was cremated in a public crematorium with little attention.

Reflections on Power and Impermanence

Power is fragile. For a kingdom that lasted a thousand years, authority, titles, and respect may seem permanent while they exist, but history knows too well how quickly they can collapse when the foundations beneath them start to wobble.

Sethu Lakshmi Bayi's story reflects that reality. The Ivory Throne is not just about queens and kings, but about the human story behind every monarchy — one that is full of insecurity, ambition, rivalry, dignity, and loss.
