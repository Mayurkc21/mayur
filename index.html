Letter
Letter to Arline
— Richard Feynman, October 17, 1946

D'Arline,

I adore you, sweetheart.

I know how much you like to hear that — but I don't only write it because you like it — I write it because it makes me warm all over inside to write it to you.

It is such a terribly long time since I last wrote to you — almost two years but I know you'll excuse me because you understand how I am, stubborn and realistic; and I thought there was no sense to writing.

But now I know my darling wife that it is right to do what I have delayed in doing, and that is to tell you I love you. I want to tell you I love you.

I find it hard to understand in my mind what it means to love you after you are dead — but I still want to comfort and take care of you — and I want you to love me and care for me. I want to have problems to discuss with you — I want to do little projects with you. I never thought until just now that we can do that. What do you think? Can we do that together? Can we do all the things we used to do together and perhaps even more? I want very much to do it with you.

What happened to the place I had reserved for you in my heart? I find it in you and in you alone. You are my only love. And I don't want to believe this is true anymore than I want to believe that you are gone. I won't let this letter end.

You, dead, are so much better than anyone else alive.

I know you will assure me that I am foolish and that you want me to have full happiness and don't want to be in my way. I'll bet you are surprised that I don't even have a girlfriend (except you, sweetheart) after two years. But you can't help it, darling, nor can I — I don't understand it, for I have met many girls and very nice ones and I don't want to remain alone — but in two or three meetings they all seem ashes. You only are left to me. You are real.

My darling wife, I do adore you.

I find it hard to make myself think that you are dead. But I believe it. If I do not mistaken we were together fourteen years, and I cannot destroy that. I was not in a position to leave even if I wanted to. It was the point that became my backbone when I had none.

I wish I could write it better. I wish I could tell you, somehow, that I am giving you something which I know you will accept — something that you are still alive to appreciate. But you are not alive and it is difficult and I am sorry.

I find myself thinking of what I was when I first met you and what I was when you left me. I was not a good person. You were a good person. And by your love, you were kind to me and by that kindness I tried to be good.

I know you won't mind if I cry a little for you. But I love you very much sweetheart.

All my love,
Your Richard

P.S. Please excuse my not mailing this — but I don't know your new address.
